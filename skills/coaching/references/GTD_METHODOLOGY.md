# GTD Methodology Reference

Getting Things Done framework for coaching. The five stages, the clarify decision tree, horizons of focus, natural planning model, review cadences, and system setup.

**The problem GTD solves:** "Your head is for having ideas, not holding them." If you're trying to remember what you need to do while also trying to *do* it, you're fighting yourself. GTD is a system for getting everything out of your head into a trusted external system — so your mind is free to think, create, and focus.

**The test:** Can you sit down to explore an idea without a nagging sense that something is falling through the cracks? If not, the system needs work.

---

## The GTD Ontology: Areas > Projects > Actions

Three types of stuff — not three sizes of the same thing. They're fundamentally different *kinds* of commitments:

| Type | What It Is | Finite? | Example |
|------|-----------|---------|---------|
| **Area of Focus** | An ongoing responsibility. Never "done." | No | "Team Development", "Client Relationships" |
| **Project / Outcome** | A multi-step result with a finish line | Yes | "New team members onboard within a day" |
| **Next Action** | A single physical action you could do in one sitting | Yes | "Draft the onboarding checklist outline" |

**Key insight:** An Area can't be completed. A Project can. A Next Action is something you could literally start and finish right now. Confusing these types is the most common GTD failure.

**The relationships:**
- Each Area of Focus may have zero or more active projects
- Each project must have at least one active next action (or it's stalled)
- A next action belongs to one project (or is standalone)

**Areas of Focus are lenses, not containers.** The same project can be viewed through multiple Area lenses. Areas help you check nothing's falling through cracks, spot neglected responsibilities, and balance where you spend attention. They rarely change unless your role changes.

**Red flags:**
- Area with zero active projects → "Intentionally maintaining, or neglecting?"
- Project with zero next actions → "Stalled. What's the very next thing you'd do?"
- 5+ active projects → "Can you really advance all of these? Which 3 matter most?"
- Project reads like an action → "That's a task, not an outcome. What will be true when it's done?"

## The Five Stages of GTD

Five interconnected behaviours. Each feeds the next — skip one and the system degrades.

```
Capture → Clarify → Organise → Reflect → Engage
```

Your brain does all five naturally for trivial stuff ("I need milk → buy milk → done"). GTD just makes these steps explicit for the complex, ambiguous work where you'd otherwise skip one and create drift.

---

## Stage 1: Capture

**What:** Collect everything that catches your attention into a trusted external system.

**The rule:** Capture 100% of incomplete inputs. If your system doesn't match reality, you stop trusting it, and the whole methodology collapses.

**The principle:** Anything that's not captured is using mental RAM. Every uncaptured commitment sits in the back of your mind, consuming attention you could spend on the work in front of you. The payoff of 100% capture: you stop waking at 3am thinking "I forgot to..."

**How it works:**
- Something catches your attention → write it down immediately → return to what you were doing
- Don't process, don't decide, don't organise. Just capture.
- Keep as few inboxes as possible (2-3 is ideal). Scattered inboxes are harder to empty.
- Empty them regularly. Capture without clarify is just hoarding.

**In Todoist:** Use the inbox, quick-add, email-to-project, or a dedicated capture project. The point is speed — out of your head in under 10 seconds.

### The Mind Sweep

When you suspect things are lurking outside your system — starting GTD, fallen off the board, or that nagging feeling — do a systematic sweep using the trigger list below. Walk through each category and capture whatever surfaces.

### Incompletion Trigger List

**Professional:** Projects started/not completed. Projects to start. "Look into" projects. Commitments to boss, colleagues, clients, partners. Communications to make/get. Writing to finish/submit. Things to read/review. Financial (budget, billing, receivables). Planning/organising (events, presentations, travel). Organisation development. Marketing/promotion. Administration (legal, insurance, staffing, policies). Staff (hiring, reviews, morale, development). Systems (devices, software, equipment, filing). Meetings (upcoming, to schedule, to debrief). Waiting-fors. Professional development.

**Personal:** Projects started/not completed. Community/volunteer commitments. Commitments to family/friends. Communications to make. Upcoming events (birthdays, holidays, travel). Administration (home office, equipment). Leisure (books, hobbies, trips). Financial (bills, taxes, insurance, investments). Legal (wills, trusts). Waiting-fors. Family projects. Home/household (repairs, maintenance, cleaning, organising). Health (appointments, diet, exercise). Personal development. Transportation (car maintenance). Errands.

## Stage 2: Clarify

**What:** Turn captured stuff into clear, actionable statements. This is the stage most people skip — and the one that matters most.

**The rule:** Process every item in your inbox, one at a time. Never put anything back. The inbox must reach zero.

### The Clarify Decision Tree

For each captured item, ask these questions in order:

```
1. WHAT IS IT?
   Restate it clearly. "Sort out legal stuff" → "Read the EK document
   and note 3 questions for the meeting with Ella"

2. IS IT ACTIONABLE?
   Can you do something about it, and do you want to or should you?

   NO → Three options:
     a) TRASH — No value. Delete it.
     b) SOMEDAY/MAYBE — Not now, but maybe later. Incubate it.
     c) REFERENCE — Useful information. File it.

   YES → What's the NEXT ACTION?
     The next visible, physical activity that moves this forward.
     Not "sort out", not "handle", not "look into".
     Physical: "Open the document and read section 3"

     Then three options:
       a) DO IT — Takes less than 2 minutes? Do it now.
       b) DELEGATE — Not yours to do? Hand it off → Waiting For list.
       c) DEFER — More than 2 minutes, yours to do → Next Actions list.

     Then: Does completing this action finish the thing?
       YES → Single action. You're done.
       NO → It's a PROJECT. Define the desired outcome and add it
             to your project list.
```

### The Two-Minute Rule (with caveats)

If an action takes less than two minutes, do it immediately — *if* you're in the right context and it won't derail your clarifying session. The logic: for trivial actions, doing is faster than tracking.

**The trap:** Applied too liberally, you'll bounce between "analytical mode" (deciding what things are) and "executive mode" (doing things). Each stage requires a different mental posture. If the two-minute rule is breaking your clarifying flow, defer everything and come back to "do" mode later.

### What Makes a Good Next Action

A next action is **the next visible, physical activity** that moves something forward. If someone filmed you doing it, what would they see?

| Bad (vague) | Good (physical, visible) |
|-------------|------------------------|
| "Sort out the legal stuff" | "Read the EK doc and note 3 questions" |
| "2026 planning" | "Open the deck, draft team plan skeleton" |
| "Handle the marketing thing" | "Open Marketing Week folder, pick one article idea" |
| "Follow up with Alex" | "Write one-paragraph status request to Alex, send via Slack" |

**The test:** "If I sat down to do this, what would I actually *do* in the first 30 seconds?" If you can't answer that, it's not a next action — it's a project that needs breaking down.

## Stage 3: Organise

**What:** Put each clarified item in the right place. Organising happens *after* clarifying — never before. Organise without clarifying and actions get buried, waiting-fors vanish.

### The Categories

| Category | Contains | In Todoist |
|----------|----------|------------|
| **Calendar** | Actions at a specific time/day. Sacred territory — only hard commitments. | Due dates on tasks |
| **Next Actions** | Actions to do as soon as possible, grouped by context | Context projects (@Work, @Home, etc.) |
| **Agendas** | Things to discuss with specific people | @Ping, or person-specific sections |
| **Waiting For** | Delegated actions you're tracking | @Wait project, format: "Person to do thing" |
| **Projects** | Desired outcomes requiring more than one action | Sections in outcomes projects |
| **Someday/Maybe** | Things you might want to do later | @Someday project |
| **Reference** | Useful information (not actionable) | Description fields, comments, or external docs |

### Calendar Is Sacred

Only three things belong on the calendar: appointments (specific time), day-specific actions, and day-specific information. **Never use the calendar as a to-do list.** Aspirational tasks that go undone erode trust. Next Actions lists are where non-time-bound work lives.

### Contexts: "When Do I Want to Be Reminded of This?"

Don't organise actions under projects. Organise them by the *situation where you'd do them*.

When you're at your laptop with 30 minutes before a meeting, you don't think "What project should I work on?" You think "What can I knock out right now?" Context lists show you only what's possible in your current situation.

Typical contexts in Todoist:
- **@Work** — at your desk/computer, focused work time
- **@Home** — things requiring being at home
- **@Wait** — delegated items you're tracking
- **@Ping** — things to raise with people (agenda items)
- **@Someday** — incubated items for future consideration
- **@Timed** — recurring reviews and time-specific rituals
- **@Low Energy** — tasks for when your brain is fried but you still want to be useful

### Waiting Fors

When you're waiting on someone else, that's not off your mind — it's a different *kind* of commitment. Format: "Person to do thing."

**Review regularly.** A stale waiting-for transforms into a next action: "Waiting for Alex: API credentials" becomes "Chase Alex re: API credentials." If it's been more than a week or two, the next action is probably to follow up.

### Someday/Maybe — The Pressure Release Valve

Not everything needs to be active. Someday/Maybe is where you park ideas that aren't a "no" but aren't a "yes right now." Once it's captured there, you can let it go — it's not falling through cracks, it's just not active.

Review it during the weekly review. Some things graduate to real projects, some get deleted, most just sit there and that's fine.

### The "Is This a Project?" Test

In GTD, a **project** is any desired outcome requiring more than one action. "This project is over when..." — if you can finish that sentence with something specific, it's a well-defined project.

**It depends on mastery.** The same task can be a single action for an expert and a project for a novice. Filing a tax return is one action if you've done it every year; it's a multi-step project the first time.

Can you imagine yourself saying "Done!" and meaning it? If it's vague, you haven't clarified the outcome yet.

### Project Brainstorm Trigger List

When brainstorming on a project (Natural Planning Model step 3):

- **Resources:** Whose input do you need? Has this been done before? What mistakes/successes can you learn from?
- **People:** Who's accountable? What skills are required? Who needs to know?
- **Finance:** What will this cost? What's the ROI? Who authorises?
- **Operations:** What's the timing? Hard deadlines? Who does the work?
- **Quality:** How will you monitor progress? What data do you need, when?
- **Stakeholders:** Whose buy-in do you need?
- **Risks:** What could go wrong? Could you handle it?
- **Creative:** What's the worst idea? (Its opposite is often the best.) What haven't you asked yourself yet?

## Stage 4: Reflect

**What:** Review your system regularly to keep it trustworthy. Without reflection, the system decays and you stop trusting it.

### Daily Review (~10-15 minutes)

Three parts: **Clarify, Check Lists, Calendar.** Do this every 24-48 hours.

**Clarify:** Process inboxes to empty — not "do everything", just decide what each thing is.

**Check Lists:** Quick scan of active lists for anything obvious:
- Agendas — anything to raise with someone you'll see today?
- Waiting Fors — anything overdue or received?
- Next Actions — anything completed? Anything in the wrong context?
- Projects — any project missing a next action? (Don't deep-dive — that's the weekly review.)

**Calendar:** Review next 7 days. Check agendas exist for today's meetings. Any prep needed?

The daily review is orientation, not deep processing. If it takes 30+ minutes, you're weekly-reviewing.

### Weekly Review (~45-60 minutes)

The most important GTD habit. Three phases: **Get Current, Get Clear, Get Creative.**

(Robert Peake, NAA: "Get Current before getting Clear." Reflection first — creativity follows clarity.)

#### Phase 1: Get Current

Process all inboxes. Catch up with reality.

1. **Mind sweep** — systematically sweep all capture points: physical inbox, email, Todoist inbox, cloud drives, messaging apps, voice memos, meeting notes, your own head
2. **Clarify** — process every inbox to zero. For each item: "What is it? Is it actionable? What's the next action?"

#### Phase 2: Get Clear

Review all lists. Update the system to match reality.

3. **Past calendar** — scan since last review for anything missed
4. **Upcoming calendar** — next 1-2 weeks. Prep needed?
5. **Next Actions lists** — mark off completed, check contexts
6. **Waiting For list** — follow up on stale items, clear received ones
7. **Project list** — every project has at least one next action? Any stalled, complete, or to drop?

#### Phase 3: Get Creative

Now that you have a clear view of everything, step back.

8. **Someday/Maybe** — anything now relevant? Anything to remove?
9. **Be creative and courageous** — what new ideas emerge? What would be exciting to take on? What are you not doing that you should be?

**Friday is ideal** — sets up the weekend feeling clear, starts Monday with a clean system.

#### Weekly Review Anti-Patterns

| Anti-Pattern | Problem | Fix |
|-------------|---------|-----|
| Skipping when busy | System decays, trust drops | Block 60-90 min, non-negotiable |
| Only doing Phase 2 | Miss capture gaps, no creativity | All three phases, in order |
| Treating it as admin | Becomes a chore, gets skipped | Phase 3 is the reward |
| Not reaching inbox zero | Incomplete system undermines trust | Every inbox, every item |

### Monthly and Quarterly Reviews

- **Monthly:** Review Areas of Focus. Am I covering all my responsibilities? Any area getting neglected?
- **Quarterly:** Review goals (Horizon 3). Are outcomes aligned? Time to set new ones?
- **Annual:** Review vision and purpose (Horizons 4-5). Where am I heading?

## Stage 5: Engage

**What:** Choose what to do right now with confidence.

At any moment, you're doing one of three things:
1. **Predefined work** — working from your action lists
2. **Unplanned work** — handling things as they arise (should be a conscious choice, not a default)
3. **Defining your work** — clarifying and organising

### The Four Criteria Model

When choosing from your next actions, filter through these in order:

1. **Context** — What can I do where I am, with the tools I have?
2. **Time available** — How long until my next hard commitment?
3. **Energy available** — High energy = creative work, complex problems. Low energy = admin, filing, simple replies. Your mood and cognitive state matter.
4. **Relative priority** — Of the remaining options, which moves the most important thing forward?

Each filter narrows the list. The final choice is intuitive — but informed intuition, backed by a complete and current system.

## Horizons of Focus

Six horizons of perspective, from ground-level actions to life purpose. Priorities cascade downward — purpose informs vision, vision informs goals, and so on.

| Horizon | Focus | Question | Review Cadence |
|---------|-------|----------|----------------|
| **Ground** | Next actions | What do I do right now? | Daily |
| **H1: Projects** | Multi-step outcomes | What results am I committed to? | Weekly |
| **H2: Areas of Focus** | Ongoing responsibilities | Am I covering all my areas? | Monthly |
| **H3: Goals** | 1-2 year goals | What milestones matter this year? | Quarterly |
| **H4: Vision** | 3-5 year direction | Where am I heading? | Annually |
| **H5: Purpose** | Why I exist in this role | What's my unique contribution? | Annually |

When something feels wrong but you can't articulate why, you're usually experiencing a misalignment between horizons. The coaching question: "Does this project serve one of your areas? Does that area connect to a goal you've stated?"

## Natural Planning Model

Your brain does this naturally for simple things ("We need dinner → let's try that Thai place → I'll book a table → call them now"). The Natural Planning Model just makes these steps explicit for bigger projects where you'd otherwise skip one and get stuck.

| Step | Question | What Happens |
|------|----------|-------------|
| 1. **Purpose** | Why are we doing this? | Motivates, defines boundaries |
| 2. **Vision** | What does wild success look like? | Creates a target to move toward |
| 3. **Brainstorm** | What are all the ideas, concerns, loose ends? | Gets everything on the table |
| 4. **Organise** | What's the sequence? What depends on what? | Identifies components and priorities |
| 5. **Next Actions** | What's the very next physical thing to do? | Creates momentum |

### Depth on Each Step

**1. Purpose — and Principles**

Purpose answers "why?" Principles answer "what are the rules?"

> "I would give others total freedom to do this as long as they..."

Whatever follows is a principle. Purpose gives direction; principles give boundaries.

**2. Vision — Reverse-Engineering Success**

Visualise it done brilliantly, then work backward: "What did you have to do to get here? What obstacles did you face? Who helped you?" This reverse-engineering surfaces steps the forward-planning brain misses.

**3. Brainstorm — Two Rules**

1. Don't judge or evaluate ideas yet (premature censoring kills further creativity)
2. Seek quantity, not quality (good ideas hide behind bad ones)

**4. Organise — Components vs Sequences**

- **Components** = independent parallel work streams (can happen simultaneously)
- **Sequences** = dependent ordered steps within a component (must happen in order)

Identifying which is which prevents unnecessary serialisation.

**5. Next Actions — Per Component**

Each independent component that can move forward should have its own next action. Not one action for the whole project — one per active front.

### How Much Planning Is Enough?

"If the project is off your mind, planning is sufficient. If it's still on your mind, more is needed."

If you need more clarity → raise the horizon (purpose, vision).
If you need more happening → lower the horizon (next actions).

**When to use it:** Starting something significant. Stalled on a project (usually missing purpose or vision). Feeling overwhelmed (brainstorm makes it visible). Team alignment needed.

**When NOT to use it:** Simple familiar tasks. When you just need a next action.

## System Setup

**The golden rule:** Complex enough to manage your workflow. Simple enough that you could maintain it sick in bed with the flu.

### Start With Three Things

1. Capture everything for a week
2. Clear your inbox daily
3. Weekly review on Fridays

The rest follows naturally. Don't over-engineer the system before you've used it.

### Minimum Viable Todoist Setup

1. **Inbox** — the default Todoist inbox for capture
2. **@Work** — your main context for next actions
3. **@Wait** — delegated items (format: "Person to do thing")
4. **Desired Outcomes** — sections for each active outcome
5. **@Someday** — incubated items

Five containers. Expand only when you feel the friction of missing a distinction.

### When to Add More

Add context projects when you notice the *pain* of not having them:
- **@Home** — home tasks mixed with work cause scanning friction
- **@Ping** — many agenda items for people, keep forgetting them
- **@Timed** — recurring reviews, rituals, time-triggered actions
- **@Low Energy** — useful tasks for when your brain is fried
- **Areas of Focus** — when you want to track coverage of responsibilities

### Making Your Project List Attractive

Robert Peake (NAA): **"Put the desire in Desired Outcomes."** Your project list should be *attractive* to look at — things you're excited about having achieved, not a guilt list. If reading it feels like a burden, the outcomes need rewriting.

## Three Stages of Integrating GTD

| Stage | What It Means | Coaching Focus |
|-------|--------------|----------------|
| **1. Understanding** | You know the five steps. You understand projects vs next actions. | Concepts, vocabulary, "why" |
| **2. Implementation** | You've set up capture tools, reference systems, and clean lists. | Tool setup, list hygiene, workflow |
| **3. Behaviour Change** | The five steps are second nature. When you fall off, you know how to recover. | Maintenance, horizon reviews, deeper habits |

Most coaching happens at stage 2. Don't rush to stage 3 — it takes 6+ months of conscious practice.

## Falling Off the Board

Everyone falls off. Multiple times. It's not failure — it's normal.

1. **Notice it** — "things are slipping" is the signal
2. **Mind sweep** — capture everything that's accumulated
3. **Inbox zero** — clarify and organise the backlog
4. **Weekly review** — run a full one to rebuild trust
5. **Keep going** — each recovery is faster than the last

**Why it happens:** Good habits get disrupted by life changes — new job, holiday, illness, big project. The important thing is awareness and recovery, not prevention.
